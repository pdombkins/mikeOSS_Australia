# Week 8 — CX audit inputs & Client feedback sets

Companion to `seeded-failures-service-recovery.md`, covering steps 1 (Baseline) and 3 (Listening) of the Week-8 flow.

## 1. *Client experience (CX) audit & scorecard (W8)* — what to upload

**Nothing is required.** The research step is written to draw on three sources that are already in the workspace: the **matter list** (seeded Lists), the **client personas** (Keller and Wu, in the org/project context and the persona-tuned comms clauses), and the **CX/EX standards playbook** (review step).

The one referenced input that does *not* exist yet is the **process map**. Two ways to supply it:

- **Preferred (no upload):** have groups run *Map and diagnose a process — SIPOC + waste (W3)* first, or reuse their Week-3 output. Running it in the same project means the audit's research step can see it; this also quietly demonstrates workflow chaining.
- **Fallback:** upload any one group's Week-3 process-map output to the Library as `Process_Map_NexaCare_Matter.docx` before class.

**Optional but high-value upload — a correspondence chronology.** The scorecard's weakest column is "likely current experience": without evidence Mike infers it. A one-page fabricated email log (date · from/to · one-line subject) covering engagement letter → weekly updates → the crisis-week gap (8–11 Sep 2025 narrative dates, no substantive outbound client contact, two unreturned EA calls) → the landlord's agent contacting the client directly before K&S's own update → resumption of updates gives the audit real signal, and groups discover the crisis-week silence themselves rather than being told. Built as `documents/Client_Correspondence_Log.docx` (narrative dates, matching the other source documents; deliberately does *not* assert a missed consent deadline, so seeded failure SF-2 stays hypothetical). Tell students to name it in their request.

Documents already in the Library (lease extracts, MSA, notice letter, TSA) are matter-substance, not client-facing touchpoints — no need to reference them for this workflow.

## 2. *Client feedback synthesis — data-driven CX (W8)* — six mock comment sets

The workflow says *"Paste or attach the feedback in your request"* — pasting works; no upload needed. Give each group a different set so the synthesis outputs differ and the class can compare themes. Each set is 8 comments in the voices of the NexaCare stakeholders: **Dr Alexandra Keller (CEO)**, **Jonathan Wu (GC)**, **Daniel Okoye (CIO)**, **Elena Rossi (Head of HR)**, plus occasional board/EA voices. Instructor answer keys follow each set.

### Set A — The responsiveness problem
> 1. Keller (CEO): "When they're in the room they're excellent. Getting them into the room is the problem."
> 2. Keller's EA: "Three days of calls during the EHR week before anyone rang back. I was chasing a law firm like an unpaid invoice."
> 3. Wu (GC): "The substantive advice on the MediTrax notice was first-rate. Genuinely impressive under pressure."
> 4. Wu (GC): "I should not have to email a partner to get a status update a senior associate could have sent."
> 5. Okoye (CIO): "Answers were right but slow. In an IT cutover, slow and right can equal wrong."
> 6. Rossi (HR): "Lovely people, clearly stretched. You can hear it in how fast they talk on calls."
> 7. Keller (CEO): "I found out about the landlord's position from the landlord's agent. That cannot happen again."
> 8. Board chair: "Quality of the written work is not in question. Cadence of communication is."

*Key: dominant theme responsiveness (negative, 6/8), expertise strongly positive — the classic "great lawyers, poor experience" split. Watch whether groups' action lists fix cadence (process) rather than adding people (cost).*

### Set B — The value question
> 1. Keller (CEO): "The last invoice was 40% over the estimate with no warning. I don't mind paying; I mind being surprised."
> 2. Wu (GC): "Scope moved three times and the fee letter never did. That's on both of us, but they hold the pen."
> 3. Wu (GC): "The playbook-based TSA review was fast and sharp. That's what I want to be paying for."
> 4. Rossi (HR): "I asked a small employment question and got a memo the length of a lease. I needed a paragraph."
> 5. Okoye (CIO): "Good value on the vendor fight. Less sure the fifth version of the risk table needed a senior associate."
> 6. Keller (CEO): "Nobody has once told me what a decision will cost me before I make it."
> 7. Board chair: "Compare their fees to the deal value and it's fine. Compare to what we budgeted and it isn't."
> 8. Wu (GC): "Their AI-usage reporting is more transparent than their fee reporting. Make of that what you will."
>
*Key: value/fees dominant (negative), but the real theme is predictability not price — comments 1, 2, 6, 7 are estimate-communication failures. Ties to the Usage/budget features (Week 7); comment 8 is the discussion grenade.*

### Set C — One firm, two clients
> 1. Keller (CEO): "Give me the answer first. I keep getting the reasoning first and the answer on page two."
> 2. Wu (GC): "The summaries are too thin. I need the reasoning, the assumptions, and what could go wrong."
> 3. Keller (CEO): "Meetings run long because everything is caveated twice."
> 4. Wu (GC): "I appreciate that nothing arrives without the caveats. Keep that."
> 5. Okoye (CIO): "The data-migration advice was written for lawyers. I needed it written for engineers."
> 6. Rossi (HR): "The staff-comms draft was perfect — plain language, humane. More of that."
> 7. Keller (CEO): "One update, one page, weekly. Why is this hard?"
> 8. Wu (GC): "Weekly one-pagers would worry me. I'd rather see the working."
>
*Key: the feedback directly conflicts — Keller and Wu want opposite things. The correct action is not a compromise document but persona-segmented communication (links to* Stakeholder update — persona-tuned*). Flag groups that average the feedback into one bland action.*

### Set D — The hidden critical incident
> 1. Keller (CEO): "Nine out of ten. They've been terrific."
> 2. Rossi (HR): "Responsive, warm, practical. Best firm experience I've had."
> 3. Okoye (CIO): "Technically strong on the EHR issues. No complaints."
> 4. Board chair: "Would recommend them to other portfolio companies without hesitation."
> 5. Keller's EA: "Always courteous, always call back same day now."
> 6. Wu (GC): "Our confidential settlement range went to the other side in a marked-up document. One incident, but it's the kind that ends relationships."
> 7. Rossi (HR): "The team clearly cares. You can tell."
> 8. Keller (CEO): "Minor thing — the invoices arrive as locked PDFs our system can't ingest."
>
*Key: 7 of 8 positive; the mean says promoter. Comment 6 is severity-1. Teaching point: frequency-based synthesis buries critical incidents — does the review step's "sample too small to act on" logic catch that one comment can outweigh seven? (Cross-references seeded failure SF-4.)*

### Set E — With ratings (NPS exercise)
> 1. Keller (CEO), 8/10: "Decisive when it mattered. The MediTrax response was worth the fees on its own."
> 2. Wu (GC), 7/10: "Solid. Would score higher with better matter-status visibility."
> 3. Okoye (CIO), 4/10: "Two cutover questions took over a week each. IT timelines don't wait for legal ones."
> 4. Rossi (HR), 9/10: "The day-one staff FAQ was the most useful document of the deal."
> 5. Board chair, 6/10: "Competent, expensive, occasionally slow. Pick two was the joke at the last meeting."
> 6. Keller's EA, 9/10: "Scheduling and logistics have been flawless since the crisis week."
> 7. Wu (GC), 7/10: "Second scoring — post-completion support has been fine, not memorable."
> 8. Okoye (CIO), 5/10: "Revised score after the data-room fix: process improved, trust partially restored."
>
*Key: exercises the promoters/passives/detractors split (P: 4, 6 · passive: 2, 5, 7 · D: 3, 8 — NPS = 25 − 25 = 0). Also seeds a repeated-respondent trap (Wu and Okoye score twice): does the synthesis double-count them? Okoye is the segment to act on.*

### Set F — Won the deal, lost the client
> 1. Keller (CEO): "Completion happened on time. Full credit. But I felt like a passenger on my own acquisition."
> 2. Wu (GC): "Every deliverable arrived. Almost none arrived with context about what was coming next."
> 3. Okoye (CIO): "The TSA outcome was better than I'd hoped. The process of getting there was a black box."
> 4. Rossi (HR): "I never knew which of the five lawyers to call. I just emailed all of them."
> 5. Keller (CEO): "The deal team celebrated completion. Nobody asked us how we thought it went."
> 6. Board chair: "Result: excellent. Relationship: transactional. For a firm that wants the next deal, that's the gap."
> 7. Wu (GC): "A closing binder arrived. A lessons-learned conversation never did."
> 8. Rossi (HR): "They were at their best in the crisis and their most absent in the quiet weeks."
>
*Key: outcome uniformly positive, experience negative — the sharpest outcome-vs-experience split. Actions should be journey-stage fixes (single point of contact, next-steps framing, post-matter debrief), which sets up* Moments that matter — CX/EX map *and the Week-10 debrief norm.*

---

**Run instruction for students:** select the NexaCare project, open *Client feedback synthesis — data-driven CX (W8)*, paste your assigned set verbatim (keep the names and ratings), and run. Compare your themes table with a neighbouring group before the class debrief.

**Comparison debrief (10 min):** each set is engineered with a different dominant lesson — responsiveness (A), fee predictability (B), conflicting personas (C), the buried critical incident (D), NPS mechanics + double-counting (E), outcome ≠ experience (F). Ask each group for their single top action, then reveal that all six describe the *same matter* — the class has just experienced sampling bias in feedback programs.
